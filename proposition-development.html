Proposition Development Framework

How we build EE One First Move — from insight to launch

This document sets out the proposition architecture, the 90-day build sequence, the internal dependencies, and the testing approach for EE One First Move — a segment proposition targeting 18-30 first-renters at the point of moving in.

Author Jean Mundabi Fala
Role context Customer Marketing Manager candidate

Builds on EE One Proposition Gap Diagnosis
01

The brief — what we are building and why

EE One is a strong product in the wrong conversation. The gap diagnosis identified three unaddressed segments, three funnel leaks, and a clear first-mover opportunity. A gap diagnosis tells us what is wrong. A proposition development paper tells us how we fix it.

The proposition we are building is called EE One First Move. It is not a new product. It is a new way of talking about, packaging, and distributing an existing one — targeted specifically at the moment a 22-year-old signs their first tenancy agreement.

Business problem

EE Consumer broadband ARPU is £41.8/month, down 1% YoY. Single-product customers are the floor. The first-renter segment — high-LTV, no incumbent, making a 24-month decision — is not being acquired at the moment of maximum intent.

Customer problem

A 22-year-old moving into their first flat has 14 things to sort in 7 days. Broadband and mobile are two separate bills, two separate customer service numbers, and two separate direct debits. They do not know a simpler option exists.

What success looks like

In 90 days: a segment landing page converting at a higher rate than the current EE One page for 18-30 visitors. A move-in channel partner identified. A business case for the move-with-us guarantee in front of product.

02

Proposition architecture

A proposition has six components. Each one must be grounded in research — not assumed. Below is the architecture for EE One First Move, built from primary source data.

Target
18 to 30 year olds moving into their first privately rented flat in the UK.
Not students in halls. Not couples buying together. Not families. Specifically: someone signing a tenancy agreement for the first time and setting up their household utilities from scratch — with or without flatmates.
The moment
The 7-day window between tenancy agreement signing and the first night in the flat.
This is when broadband and mobile decisions are made. Before this window: no urgency. After it: they have already committed to a provider for 24 months. EE One needs to be the first conversation, not the catch-up call.
The promise
"Your flat sorted. One account. One bill. Broadband live by move-in day."
The promise is certainty and simplicity — not price. At the move-in moment, a first-renter's primary anxiety is not saving £5/month. It is: will broadband actually be live when I need it? EE One owns that answer. No competitor does.
Proof points
Three things EE can actually commit to that competitors cannot.
See Section 3 for detail on each. The distinction matters: these are not features. They are claims — things EE can say and defend that no other provider can say in the same breath.
Pricing frame
Show the total household cost, not the per-person cost.
EE One from £41.99 sounds expensive against Vodafone's £24 broadband headline. But EE One covers broadband + mobile. A household of 3 on EE One pays £41.99 + £24 in flatmate SIMs = £65.99 for three people. That is £22/person. The comparison site frame loses. The household calculator wins.
Name and frame
EE One First Move.
Not a new brand. An extension of EE One — the word "First" does double duty: first flat, first move, first-renter. The proposition sits inside EE One, not beside it. This matters for brand architecture: we are not creating a sub-brand, we are naming a segment moment.
03

The three proof points — and why they are defensible

Proof points are not marketing claims. They are commitments the product, commercial, and operations teams can actually back. Each one below is matched to an internal owner, a gap it closes, and the reason it is differentiated in the market.

Proof point 1 — Move-in priority installation

Broadband live within 5 working days of the key collection date — not 5 days from order. The installation is anchored to when the customer moves in, not when the order is processed. Every competitor's installation window is system-driven. EE's can be customer-driven.

Why defensible: This requires an operational commitment from the install team, not a product change. The commercial case is clear — a first-renter who gets broadband live on day one has a dramatically different first-month NPS than one who waits 14 days.

Proof point 2 — Flatmate SIMs from £12/month, added in one go

The household SIM discount already exists inside EE One. This proof point is about packaging and framing. "Add your flatmates to your EE One account from £12/month each. One account. No separate contracts." The calculator shows the combined household saving vs paying separately — £327/year for a household of three.

Why defensible: No other UK provider has a bundled mobile-for-flatmates offer that sits inside a broadband contract. Virgin Volt is individual. Vodafone Together saves £4/month. This saves £27/month at household level.

Proof point 3 — Move-with-us guarantee

When you move home within the 24-month term, your contract terms stay the same. No new contract. No price reset. No early exit fee if you are moving to an area EE cannot serve. This is a policy change, not a product change. It reverses EE's single biggest churn trigger in the 18-30 segment and turns the forum's most visible complaint category into a competitive differentiator.

Why defensible: No competitor offers it. The fact that EE's current policy is the opposite makes the switch genuinely newsworthy. This is a PR story as well as a proposition claim — "EE becomes the first UK broadband provider to guarantee contract continuity when you move home."

Important: None of these proof points require new infrastructure. They require internal alignment, a policy decision on home-move contract handling, and a repackaging of the household SIM discount. The proposition can be built before the product changes — and the product changes can follow the evidence from conversion data.
04

Positioning — what we own and what we do not fight for

Positioning is not about being the best on every dimension. It is about choosing which dimension to win on and making everything else irrelevant. EE One First Move cannot win on headline price — Vodafone's £24 broadband entry point is lower. It should not try.

The dimension EE One First Move wins on is certainty at the move-in moment. This is a dimension no competitor is currently competing on. Nobody owns "your first flat, sorted." The market is fighting for price comparison clicks. EE One First Move exits that fight entirely.

05

The 90-day build — four phases

Proposition development is not a single launch. It is a sequence of bets, each one building evidence for the next. The logic here is: test cheap before building expensive. Start with what can be validated on existing infrastructure, then fund the bigger builds with conversion data.

Wks 01–03 Validate the insight

Before we build anything, we confirm the assumptions

T1Pull the NPS cut for 18-30 new EE One customers from the data warehouse. Split by those who moved in the last 3 months vs those who did not. What is the gap in satisfaction? This is the first data point.
T2Quantify the moving-home churn rate in the 18-30 cohort. How many customers move home annually? Of those, what percentage lapse vs renew? What is the revenue at risk? This is the commercial case for the move-with-us guarantee.
T3Pull search entry data for ee.co.uk/ee-one. What percentage of 18-30 visitors come from comparison sites vs direct search vs organic? What queries are driving them? This tells us whether we are in the right conversation.
T4Run 3 qualitative interviews with recent 18-30 EE One customers who moved in the last 6 months. One hour each. Specific question: "Walk me through the week before and after you moved in. What were you trying to sort? When did you think about broadband?"
Output: a data pack with the commercial case for EE One First Move — used in Phase 2 stakeholder alignment
Wks 04–06 Build the case internally

Get the internal yes — proposition, policy, and partnership

T5Write the internal proposition brief: target, moment, promise, proof points, pricing frame, channel strategy, success metrics. One page. This is the document that aligns brand, product, and commercial before anything is built.
T6Brief Rachel Ward's product team on the move-with-us guarantee. Present the commercial case: moving-home churn cost, community forum sentiment data, competitive gap analysis. Specific ask: change the home-move trigger from "new 24-month contract" to "contract continuation" for customers within existing term.
T7Scope the SpareRoom partnership. SpareRoom has 20 million users, zero competing broadband partner, and a tenancy confirmation email that goes to every new tenant. First meeting: what does a co-branded move-in moment integration cost vs the value of intercepting a first-renter at maximum intent?
T8Brief the creative team on EE One First Move. The creative territory: certainty, not savings. The visual world: the first night in a new flat — keys on the counter, boxes everywhere, phone in hand. Human, specific, not stock photography.
Output: signed-off proposition brief + product team alignment on move-with-us policy + SpareRoom commercial terms
Wks 07–09 Test before building

Validate the proposition with real visitors before full launch

T9Run a 3-variant A/B test on the current EE One hero headline. No new page required — swap the headline only. Variant A: "The perfect plan for your household" (control). Variant B: "Your first flat. One bill. Broadband live by move-in day." Variant C: "Sort your whole flat in one go." Measure: bounce rate and "Create EE One" click rate for 18-30 visitors.
T10Add the flatmate household calculator to the existing EE One page as a new section. No redesign. Measure: interaction rate and conversion lift for visitors who use it vs those who scroll past.
T11Send a single CRM email to existing 18-30 EE mobile customers on student plans who have not taken broadband. Subject: "Moving out of uni?" One clear offer. Measure: open rate, click rate, and EE One conversion from this cohort.
Output: conversion data for each variant — informs which headline, which framing, and which channel to invest in at launch
Wks 10–12 Launch and measure

Go live with the evidence behind us

T12Publish the EE One First Move segment landing page — built on winning headline and framing from Phase 3 test. 15 sections. Savings calculator. Flatmate SIM mechanic. Move-with-us guarantee section. Distinct URL for tracking.
T13Launch SEO content programme: 4 articles targeting "broadband when moving flat UK", "how long does broadband take to set up", "best broadband for first flat", "how to add flatmates to broadband account." These are informational queries that comparison sites do not rank for. EE can own them.
T14Pilot the SpareRoom integration — if commercial terms are agreed — with a co-branded tenancy confirmation email. Measure: click-through to segment landing page and conversion to EE One sign-up vs standard acquisition channel CAC.
T15Brief PR on the move-with-us guarantee — if policy is confirmed. Pitch angle: "EE becomes the first UK broadband provider to guarantee your contract when you move home." Target: consumer tech, personal finance, and tenants' rights press. This is earned media that comparison site campaigns cannot generate.
Output: live segment page + first 30 days of conversion data + SpareRoom pilot results + PR coverage on move-with-us guarantee

07

How we know the proposition is working

Every metric below maps to a specific proposition claim. If the proposition is right, these numbers move. If they do not, we have a specific question to investigate — not a vague performance problem.

Primary
Segment page conversion rate vs EE One control
Track "Create EE One" clicks for 18-30 visitors on the First Move segment page vs the existing EE One page. Isolate by age band using analytics. Run as a controlled split test before full redirect.
Target: +20% conversion rate vs control within 60 days of launch
Primary
ARPU of segment page acquisitions vs generic page
Do customers acquired through the First Move page take flatmate SIMs at a higher rate? If the proposition is working, segment page acquisitions should have higher 90-day ARPU than generic EE One acquisitions.
Target: First Move acquisition ARPU 40% above single-product ARPU within 90 days
Secondary
Moving-home churn rate in 18-30 cohort
Baseline: measure the % of 18-30 EE customers who lapse within 30 days of changing their address on the account. After the move-with-us guarantee is live, track the same cohort. This is the retention signal for Proof Point 3.
Target: 25% reduction in move-triggered churn within 6 months of guarantee launch
Secondary
Flatmate SIM attach rate on new EE One accounts
Of all new EE One accounts opened by 18-30 customers, what percentage add at least one flatmate SIM within 30 days? This measures whether the flatmate packaging is landing — not just converting broadband, but converting the household.
Target: 18% SIM attach rate on segment page acquisitions vs current baseline
Leading indicator
Bounce rate reduction on 18-30 EE One visitors
Current EE One page has no first-renter framing. 18-30 visitors bounce because they see nothing for them. After the headline A/B test and segment page launch, measure bounce rate change for this cohort specifically.
Target: bounce rate down 15 percentage points within 30 days of headline test
Leading indicator
Calculator interaction rate
The savings calculator is the most direct measure of proposition engagement. If visitors use it, the framing is working. If they scroll past, the entry point into the calculator is wrong. Track interaction rate and completion rate separately.
Target: 22% of page visitors interact with calculator within 45 days of launch
08

The test sequencing — cheapest signals first

The order of tests matters. We build evidence from low-cost, low-commitment tests before investing in new infrastructure. Each test below can be run on existing pages with no new development — and each one answers a specific proposition question before the next investment is made.

01

Hero headline A/B — existing EE One page

Swap the headline only. Three variants. Measure click rate and bounce rate for 18-30 visitors. Question answered: which framing resonates — certainty, simplicity, or savings? This determines the hero for the segment landing page and for all downstream creative.

Low effort
02

Flatmate calculator — added to existing EE One page

Add a single new section — the household savings calculator — to the existing page. No redesign. Measure interaction rate and whether users who interact convert at a higher rate. Question answered: does showing the household saving change conversion, or does it not matter?

Low effort
03

CRM email to student SIM customers

One email to the 18-30 cohort on student SIM plans who do not have broadband. Subject line: "Moving out of uni? Your EE One is ready." Question answered: is the student-to-first-renter pipeline viable, or does the cohort not convert at meaningful rates?

Low effort
04

Segment landing page — full build

Only after Tests 01–03 have validated the headline, the flatmate framing, and the CRM channel. Build the full segment page with the winning creative treatment. This is where the budget goes — informed by real conversion data, not assumptions.

Medium effort
05

SpareRoom pilot — tenancy confirmation email

Co-branded email to new SpareRoom tenants within 24 hours of tenancy confirmation. Links to segment landing page. Question answered: what is the CAC from move-in moment targeting vs search and comparison sites? This becomes the commercial case for a deeper SpareRoom integration.

Medium effort
The underlying principle: a proposition that requires a full re-platform to validate is too expensive to test. Every test here runs on existing infrastructure. By Week 9, we have data on five separate proposition hypotheses — before a single pound of additional paid media is committed.
