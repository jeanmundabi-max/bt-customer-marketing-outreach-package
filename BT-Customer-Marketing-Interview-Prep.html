Study Sequence

  1. Read the Cheat Sheet — internalise the commercial context and the ARPU/churn numbers
  2. Read the Pre-Interview Analysis (Cards 12-13) — your single biggest differentiator over external candidates
  3. Rehearse Tell Me About Yourself until it is under 2 minutes
  4. Rehearse one Primary STAR per competency — out loud, timed
  5. Run through Mock Pushbacks the night before
  6. Sleep. Do not cram on the morning.

Golden Rules

  • Every answer ends with a BT Consumer hook — name something specific about their commercial situation
  • Results must have numbers — "market differentiation" is an output, not a result. Tie it to something measurable.
  • Pause before answering — one breath. Signals confidence, not panic.
  • Lead with action in every STAR — the longest section should be A (how you worked), not S (context)
  • If you don't know something, say so — "I'd want to validate that with customer data first" beats a bluff every time
  • Active voice throughout — "I led", "I built", "I presented" — not "we" or "the team"
The internal candidate trap: Do not rely on "I already work here" as your main pitch. Use it as a multiplier. "My Santander proposition work is directly relevant AND I already understand how BT makes decisions" — both, not just the second.

The proposition launched with Ant and Dec as its brand anchor. The partnership became the centrepiece of Santander's mass-market current account strategy for the following two years.

Proposition EE One for First-Renters — Activating an Existing Product in the Right Moment
▾

What Already Exists (Do Not Claim to Invent This)

EE One — when a customer has EE broadband and EE mobile on the same account, they unlock household SIM discounts: up to £20/month off additional mobile SIMs, unlimited data boost on eligible existing plans, no cap on number of SIMs. The mechanic is already there. It is not being activated for the 18-30 first-renter segment.

The Actual Gap — Why EE One Is Not Reaching This Segment

Gap 1 — Wrong moment, wrong channel
EE One is marketed as a generic household saving. It is invisible at the decision moment.

When a 23-year-old signs a tenancy on SpareRoom, they Google "cheap broadband for renters" within 48 to 72 hours. EE One does not appear in that search. Virgin Volt does. The product exists — the channel targeting does not. No contextual presence on SpareRoom, Rightmove, or any rental platform at the moment of the broadband decision.

Gap 2 — The billing blocker
EE One puts all SIMs under one account and one bill. In a flatshare, that is a deal-breaker.

The person who sets up the broadband becomes the billing account holder for every flatmate's mobile plan. No 23-year-old wants to be on the hook for their flatmates' phone bills. EE One structurally requires one person to take financial responsibility for the whole household. There is no split-billing mechanic — each flatmate pays their own mobile plan while still benefiting from the shared broadband discount. This one friction point stops EE One from being adopted in flatshare households, which is the primary living situation of the 18-30 target segment.

Gap 3 — No segment identity
EE One is a tariff name, not a proposition with a clear audience.

It is not packaged or named for the moment of a first rental. It does not speak to the "moving in with friends" context. The emotional landing for a 23-year-old is not "household savings" — it is "sort your flat in one go." The product mechanics are right. The packaging, messaging and naming are not built for this segment.

The Proposition: EE One, Activated for First-Renters

Not a new product. Three specific additions to make EE One land with the 18-30 segment:

  1. Move-in moment targeting — contextual presence on SpareRoom and Rightmove at the point of rental search and tenancy signing. "Found your flat? Sort your broadband and get your flatmates' phones covered at the same time." One CTA, one moment.
  2. Split-billing mechanic — each flatmate's EE mobile plan links to the shared broadband account for the EE One discount but pays their own bill independently. The broadband account holder gets the household discount unlocked. Everyone else pays their own phone bill. The financial liability is personal, the commercial benefit is shared. This is a product build (Rachel Ward's domain) — the Customer Marketing brief needs to define this as a hard requirement for the proposition to work in a flatshare context.
  3. First-renter packaging — name it clearly for the audience. "EE One — Move In" or equivalent. Position it not as a tariff but as a life moment solution: "Everything your flat needs, from one provider, without the admin."
